Print Material Guide

Resin or filament.

Mini-frets take direct sliding wear from strings. Pick based on string type and how often you're willing to reprint.

Metal strings
Hardened / tough resin

Best wear resistance of the practical options. Steel strings groove plastic fast — expect reprints roughly every 2 weeks with regular play, sooner with heavy bending. Standard resin wears too quickly; use a tough/hard formulation.

Nylon strings
Resin or nylon (PA) filament

Nylon strings are far gentler — either option lasts noticeably longer than the metal-string case. PETG becomes usable here too, since the string itself isn't abrasive.

Bass strings (metal)
Hardened / tough resin, size up

Worse than guitar metal strings — thicker gauge and higher tension means more force per contact point. Same material choice as metal guitar strings, but expect faster wear; consider printing frets slightly taller/thicker than the guitar spec to compensate.

Installation

Gluing the frets down.

Bottom is flat, sits flush on the fretboard surface — clean contact face, no embedding.

Resin / PA frets (reprint often)
Medium CA glue

Strong enough to hold under string tension, sets fast, but releases with heat or acetone when you need to swap a worn piece out. Clamp or hold each segment ~10-20 seconds while it sets.

Metal frets (permanent)
2-part epoxy

Longer working time to align each piece before it sets, won't creep or fatigue like CA over years. Not meant to be removed — use only once positions are confirmed.

Either way: dry-fit every piece first using the exported STL positions before applying glue — the stepped geometry only works if each segment lands exactly where computed.

Resin

Finest detail — needed at 1.4mm fret scale. Hard/tough resin recommended over standard.

PETG

Fine for nylon strings. Wears fast under steel strings — avoid there.

Nylon (PA) filament

Tougher than PETG, lower friction. Use 0.2mm nozzle — 1.2mm fret width is tight for 0.4mm.

No real long-term wear data exists for this geometry — estimates based on general plastic-vs-steel sliding wear. Test one, observe actual grooving rate for your playing style.
Metal frets

STL works for metal too.

Same STL file, no conversion needed — metal 3D printing (DMLS/SLM) reads standard STL like resin and filament do.

Desktop printers don't do metal — this gets outsourced to a print service. For a part this small, though, real fret wire (brass/nickel silver/stainless) is likely cheaper and simpler than paying for metal printing. Worth comparing before committing.
