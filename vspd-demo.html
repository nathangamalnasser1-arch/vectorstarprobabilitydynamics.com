Rigorous Framework

In Hilbert Space, completeness requires the Lebesgue integral: every Cauchy sequence of square-integrable functions converges to a limit within the space. The wave function \(|\psi\rangle\) during a measurement window \(\Delta t\) is represented as a sum over orthonormal basis elements (e.g., sine/cosine in Fourier decomposition). For the state to remain a valid wave function throughout \(\Delta t\), the sequence of partial sums must converge in the \(L^2\) norm‚Äîi.e., the Cauchy condition must hold so that the limit lies in the same Hilbert Space.

The apparent "spread" of \(|\psi|^2\) with increasing \(\Delta t\) reflects the inclusion of more micro-path vectors \(\vec{v}_i\) in the observable superposition \(\Psi_{\text{obs}} = \sum_i \vec{v}_i\), each contributing from orthonormal directions in the space.

Mathematical Resolution

1. The Redshift Engine

\[ z = \frac{\lambda_{\text{observed}} - \lambda_{\text{rest}}}{\lambda_{\text{rest}}} \]

This is the mechanistic proof of time dilation: light emitted in a gravitational well is redshifted when observed from outside. The Hubble Space Telescope measures this via Balmer lines (e.g., H\(\alpha\)). Larger \(z\) means stronger gravitational time dilation‚Äîthe "engine" that powers the Time Microscope.

2. The Vector-Star Summation

\[ \Psi_{\text{obs}} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \vec{v}_i \]

\(\vec{v}_i\) are individual micro-path vectors in Hilbert Space that contribute to the observed state during the \(\Delta t\) interval. As \(\Delta t\) increases, \(n\) grows and the superposition includes more vectors, producing the broadened \(|\psi|^2\).

3. The Hilbert Space Inner Product

\[ \langle f \mid g \rangle = \int f^*(x)\, g(x)\, dx \]

This inner product measures "distance" and "angles" between state vectors. The probability density \(|\psi|^2\) is obtained from \(\langle \psi \mid \psi \rangle\); it quantifies how much of the state lies along each basis direction and thus the observable spread during the measurement window.

The Mechanistic Proof: Sirius B

Sirius B is a white dwarf with a very high mass-to-radius ratio. That ratio creates a deep gravitational well at its surface. General relativity predicts that clocks run slower in stronger gravitational fields‚Äîso spectral lines emitted from Sirius B's atmosphere are gravitationally redshifted when we observe them from Earth.

Hubble spectroscopy measures this redshift in Balmer lines (e.g., H\(\alpha\), H\(\beta\)). The measured \(z\) matches the predicted value from \(z \approx GM/(c^2 R)\) for the star's mass \(M\) and radius \(R\). This is the mechanistic proof: the same time dilation that produces the redshift can be interpreted in VSPD as a "Time Microscope"‚Äînear Sirius B, the effective \(\Delta t\) for a local measurement is reduced, sharpening the wave function and reducing the apparent quantum blur.
